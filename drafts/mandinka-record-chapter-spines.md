**CHAPTER 2 — WORDS AND SPELLING**

*Core text. Grade 8\. Never changes.*

You already know this language. You have spoken it your whole life. Every word in this chapter lives in your mouth before it ever touches a page.

The challenge is not the language. The challenge is the translation from sound to symbol. That is a skill. And like every skill, it improves with practice. Difficulty here is not failure. It is a pathway being built for the first time.

**Why spelling feels hard —** Your brain learned this language through sound, rhythm, and repetition — starting before you could walk. Reading and spelling use a different pathway. That pathway has to be trained. Even fluent English speakers sometimes struggle to spell a word they have said a thousand times. This is not a sign of weakness. It is how the human brain works.

**The Two Charts**

You have two tools. Use them every time you are unsure.

**Chart 1 — Consonants —** When you cannot hear which letter to write, feel where your mouth is. Where are your lips? Where is your tongue? Your mouth already knows the sound. The chart connects the mouth position to the letter.

**Chart 2 — Vowels —** Short sound — one letter — one clap. Long sound — two letters — clap and hold. If you hear O, write O. If you hold it — OO. Your body knows the length. Write what you feel.

**What the AI handles —** You do not need to mark tone. You do not need to mark nasalization. You do not need to write special characters for implosive sounds. Write the closest base letter you hear. Yahura will listen to your recording and add the marks that are needed. Your job is consistency. Write the same sound the same way every time. That is enough.

**Your goal: collect the words for this domain. Write them in your language. Write them in English. Record yourself saying them. You are building the vocabulary of this book — words that did not exist in written form before you wrote them.**

**CHAPTER 4 — CREATIVE WRITING**

*Core text. Grade 8\. Never changes.*

You are now an author.

Everything in the first three chapters — the words you collected, the sentences you built — that is your material. This chapter is where you make something with it.

Authors do not just report facts. They make choices. Every word, every comparison, every sound — chosen deliberately to make the reader feel something before they understand it. These choices have names. Learning the names gives you control over tools you already use when you speak.

**Literary Devices — How authors build meaning**

**Symbolism —** using an object or image to represent something larger than itself. A key can represent freedom. A broken branch can represent loss. A bird in flight can represent hope. The symbol carries meaning beyond what it literally is.

**Metaphor —** saying one thing IS another thing, without using like or as. A person's reputation is a flying bird. Time is a river. The language is a root. Metaphor does not compare. It declares. It says: these two things are the same thing seen from a different angle.

**Simile —** saying one thing is LIKE another thing. The voice moves like water. Patience is like a tree — it grows slowly but holds everything. Simile keeps the two things separate but shows how they touch.

**Personification —** giving human qualities to something that is not human. The wind whispered. The tree remembers. The river refuses to go back. This is one of the oldest tools in oral tradition. The world speaks. Everything has a voice.

**Imagery —** using detailed, sensory description to create a picture in the reader's mind. Not just the bird sang, but a sharp, clear note cut through the morning air before anyone else was awake. Imagery makes the reader see, hear, feel, smell, and taste. It puts them inside the moment.

**Juxtaposition —** placing two opposite things next to each other so the contrast does the work. A small axe can fell a big tree. United we stand, divided we fall. The difference between the two things IS the meaning.

**Parallelism —** using the same structure twice or more to create balance and emphasis. We came with nothing. We leave with a record. The elder speaks. The student writes. The book remembers.

**Hyperbole —** deliberate exaggeration to make a point stronger than the literal truth. I am so hungry I could eat a horse. I have told you a thousand times. Hyperbole does not lie. It intensifies.

**Alliteration —** the same sound starting multiple words close together. The sound itself carries meaning. The repetition of the first sound holds the line together.

**Poetic Devices — How poets build music**

**Call and Response —** one voice offers a line, the group answers. The call opens. The response closes. Together they make something neither could make alone. This is one of the oldest structures in human music and speech. The speaker leads. The community completes.

**Praise Singing —** listing the names, deeds, and qualities of a person or thing, building in intensity with each line. The repetition is not redundant — it is cumulative. Each line adds weight. Each name adds truth. This is how the spoken record was kept before writing existed.

**Rhyme —** matching sounds at the end of lines, or echoing inside a line. Listen for where the sounds return.

**Rhythm —** the beat of the words. Fast or slow. Heavy or light. Clap while you read your work. Feel where the beat falls.

**Assonance —** repeated vowel sounds inside words. When the same vowel sound appears across several words in a line it creates music without rhyme.

**Onomatopoeia —** words that sound like what they describe. The crack of a branch. The whistle of a bird. The word carries the sound inside it.

**Repetition —** returning to a word or phrase to give it weight. Repetition is not weakness. It is emphasis with rhythm.

**Your Task**

**Beneath each line, name the device you used. That naming is the skill. That is what makes the difference between a student who wrote something and an author who made a choice.**

**CHAPTER 5 — OUR ORAL TRADITIONS**

*Core text. Grade 8\. Never changes.*

Before there was writing, there was the voice.

For hundreds of thousands of years, human beings kept all their knowledge in sound — in stories, songs, proverbs, and the careful memory of people trained to hold it. The Griot. The elder. The keeper of names. This was not a lesser form of knowledge. It was the original form. The most tested. The most trusted.

Writing is powerful. But it is young. And it cannot do everything the voice can do. Tone. Pause. Breath. The weight a word carries when it is spoken by someone who has lived it — that does not transfer easily to a page. This chapter is about learning to carry it anyway.

**What oral tradition is —** Oral tradition is not just old stories. It is a living system for holding and passing knowledge. Every proverb is a compressed lesson. Every praise song is a record of history. Every riddle is a way of teaching people to think. These are technologies — as precise and functional as any written text, and in some ways more durable.

**What we are doing —** We are not preserving oral tradition by putting it in a museum. We are extending it — giving it a new form that can travel further and last longer without losing what matters. When you write a proverb in your language, you are not replacing the oral form. You are giving it another life. When you record an elder's voice and transcribe what they say, you are creating a bridge between the spoken and the written that did not exist before.

You have spent four weeks collecting words, building sentences, and writing original work. You are now an author. This week you become a junior publisher.

A publisher does not just write. A publisher asks: who is this for? What form should it take? What will the reader see, feel, and hold? These are not small questions. Every book that has ever been made began here — with someone deciding what kind of thing it would be.

**Our Book Is 32 Pages**

That is the standard for children's books around the world. Not because 32 is a magic number, but because it is what fits in a binding, what a child can hold, and what a printer can produce at a cost that makes sense. Every picture book you have ever seen follows this structure.

**Who Is Reading It?**

Every children's book has two readers. The child who holds it and the adult who chooses it. The child wants to be entertained, to see themselves in the story, to hear language that moves. The adult — a parent, a teacher, a Ministry official — wants to know it is safe, valuable, and worth keeping. Your book must speak to both. That is not a compromise. It is a skill.

**What Kind of Book Is It?**

Books for young readers come in different forms. Some are picture books where the illustration carries as much meaning as the words. Some are story books where a longer narrative unfolds across the pages. Some teach a concept — colours, numbers, the names of things. Some tell a story with a lesson inside it.

Your instructor will help you decide which form fits the work you have done. The words you collected, the sentences you built, the written work you created — those tell us what kind of book wants to be made.

**Pictures and Words**

In a picture book, the illustration and the text do not repeat each other. The picture shows what the words do not say. The words say what the picture cannot show. Together they make something neither could make alone. Think about what your book needs the reader to see. Think about what it needs them to hear.

**Plot**

Every story moves. Something happens. It causes something else. That leads somewhere. The reader follows because they want to know where it goes.

The simplest plot has five moments: Something begins. Something is wanted or needed. Something gets in the way. Something changes. Something ends.

Your domain and your culture. The plot of your book lives somewhere in the space between those two things. Where it lands is yours to discover.

**Protecting Your Recordings**

Every recording you make is a primary source. It is research-grade data. It deserves to be named clearly, stored carefully, and annotated so it can be found and used again.

**Name your files clearly —** Sound recording: \[Subject Name\] \+ Sound \+ \[Date\]. Interview: \[Speaker Name\] \+ \[Subject\] \+ \[Date\]. A file with no name is a voice that cannot be found.

**Annotate every recording —** For each recording, document: the speaker's name and role in the community; the audio file reference; the spoken text written in your language; the English translation; the meaning or lesson it carries; your personal reflection on what you heard.

**Present your findings —** Play your recordings. Let the voice be heard in the room where the knowledge was always shared. That is the point of everything you have built.

**Your Task**

Find one proverb, one riddle, or one short story from your domain. Write it in your mother tongue. Write it in English. Write one paragraph explaining what it means and when it is used.

**This is the oral tradition entering the written record — through you.**

**CHAPTER 6 — THE VILLAGE ELDERS**

*Core text. Grade 8\. Never changes.*

An elder is anyone who knows something you do not yet know.

That might be a grandmother. A farmer. A hunter who has watched this domain for sixty years. A thirty-two year old who has spent their life at the river and knows every species that comes through. Age is not the measure. Knowledge is the measure. Your job is to find who holds it and ask them to share it with you.

**The conversation must be in your language —** The whole interview happens in your mother tongue and it must be recorded. Listen for one moment — ten to twenty seconds, two or three sentences — that feels true and important. Write it down using your two spelling charts. Translate it for meaning. That recording and that written text go to Yahura. That is where the voice becomes the record.

**Who you are meeting —** A subject matter expert is anyone who holds knowledge you do not yet have. In this domain that might be someone who has watched the land for thirty years, someone who knows which signs signal the seasons, an elder who remembers the proverb and the story behind it, or someone who knows both the local name and the science in between.

**What you bring —** You are not arriving empty-handed. You are arriving as an author with work to share. Show them what you have written. Read what you created. Say the words you collected. Ask them if you got it right. Ask them what you missed. Ask them what they would add. This is not an interview where you take and they give. It is a conversation between people who both know something. You know how to write it down. They know what is true.

**What you are really doing —** Every skill you have built in this workbook — spelling, grammar, creative writing, oral tradition — you are using right now in the real world. With real people. For a real purpose. This is what those skills are for. Not the exam. This.

**How to approach an elder —** Respect is not just politeness. It is the method. An elder who feels respected will give you more than you asked for. An elder who feels rushed or dismissed will give you nothing. Greet properly. Explain what you are doing and why. Ask if they are willing. Accept their answer — yes or no — with the same respect. If they say yes, listen more than you speak. Do not interrupt. Do not correct. Your opinion about their knowledge is not relevant. Your job is to receive it.

**How to ask good questions —** Bad questions close conversations. Good questions open them. Do not ask a question that can be answered in one word. Ask instead what people say about the subject, what it means, what it signals, what it warns, what it celebrates. Ask what your elder remembers hearing about it when they were young.

**How to record and cite —** Record and write down exactly what they say as closely as you can. Use your two spelling charts. Record audio only if they give permission. When you write it in your book, write their name, their role in the community, and where and when you spoke with them. They are an author too. Their contribution belongs to them.

**The people you meet today may one day be cited in the book you publish. Their knowledge, acknowledged by name, entering the permanent record. That is what it means to be taken seriously as an author.**

**After the Interview — Questions to Ask Yourself**

The interview is not over when the recording stops. What you carry back matters as much as what you captured. Use these questions to process what happened.

**On identity —** Before this project, your name was just a word. Now that you know something of the history and meaning behind what you carry — does it feel different when you say it out loud? After sitting with someone who holds deep knowledge, do you feel you are living only for yourself — or are you now carrying something with you?

**On language —** What words or expressions did you encounter in this domain that you had never heard before? Why do you think those words are disappearing from your generation? Were there things you heard that were impossible to translate without losing something? What feeling was lost?

**On the experience —** What was the one thing you heard that completely changed how you see something familiar? Was there a moment of silence during the interview? What was happening in that silence? Do you feel closer to the knowledge of your community than before you began?

**On the future —** Now that you have these recordings, how will you make sure they survive? The next time there is a disagreement or difficulty in your community, will what you learned change how you approach it?

**CHAPTER 7 — PUBLISHING AND MEDIA**

*Core text. Grade 8\. Never changes.*

You are about to publish a book.

Not one day. Not when you are older. Now.

**What you have —** Words collected in your language and in English. Sentences that demonstrate how your language works. A poem, a proverb, a story — original work that belongs to you. Knowledge gathered from a subject matter expert, cited and attributed. A 32-page plan for a book built around your domain. That is a manuscript. That is the raw material of a published work.

**What publishing means —** Publishing means making your work available to others in a controlled, permanent form. Controlled means you decide who can use it and how. Permanent means it does not disappear when the conversation ends or the class is over. Publishing is not just for famous people. It is for anyone with something to say worth keeping.

**What a Book Is Made Of**

Every book has the same basic components. You have already made them all.

Content — the words, sentences, stories, and knowledge. You wrote this.

Structure — the chapters, the order, the sections. This workbook provided that.

Attribution — whose work it is and where the knowledge came from. You documented this.

Copyright — the legal statement of ownership. AI West Africa registers this on your behalf.

ISBN — a unique number that puts this book in the global record of published works. Every book in The Mandinka Record has one.

**What Happens After Publication**

Your work enters a governed system. AI West Africa manages the licensing — who can use your work and under what conditions. When your work generates revenue — through educational licensing, AI training data agreements, research use, or any other channel — you receive your share. This is tracked. This is enforced. This is your right.

Your name goes on the cover. The people you interviewed are credited inside. The copyright is registered. An ISBN is assigned — a unique number that places this book in the global record of published works forever.

**What This Book Is Part Of**

Your book is one volume in The Mandinka Record. A series that will grow, domain by domain, cohort by cohort, for as long as students like you choose to add to it.

The next cohort will read what you wrote. They will build on it. They will correct what needs correcting and expand what deserves to grow. That is how a record works. It never closes.

**In the Mirror — A Final Reflection**

Before you complete the publishing template, sit with what you built over seven weeks. This is where raw data becomes something more. These questions are not for an exam. They are for you.

**The object of memory —** If everything you learned in these seven weeks could be held in one hand as a single object, what would it be? A seed. A stone. A feather. A thread. Name it. That object is the heart of your book.

**The map —** Draw the path the knowledge you collected has traveled. Where did it begin? Who held it before you? Where does it live now? How does seeing that map change how you feel about the work you did?

**The translation challenge —** Take one proverb or term you recorded from your domain. Try to translate it into a modern context — a message, a caption, a sentence a person your age would understand. How do you carry the meaning without losing the soul of the word?

**The oath —** You now carry knowledge that did not exist in written form before you wrote it down. What is your responsibility to it? Who is the first person you will tell? What is the first thing you will do to make sure it survives?

By the end of this process you should be able to say three things clearly:

Every chapter follows the same format. No exceptions. No stories. No extra paragraphs. The format is the discipline.

**Entry Format:**

**\[Mandinka Proverb — exact spelling, exact words\]**

*\[English translation — literal first, then natural\]*

**What this means:** \[1–3 sentences explaining the life lesson this proverb carries. Write in clear, respectful language a younger reader can understand. Do not simplify the meaning — find words that carry it accurately.\]

**Action — Editing Pass 1: Mandinka Accuracy**

* Is every Mandinka word spelled correctly?  
* Is the vowel length right? (Ba vs. Baa — check your phonetic notes from Week 3.)  
* Is the proverb written exactly as the elder said it, or as the class agreed it should be standardized?  
* If any word is uncertain, mark it \[VERIFY\] and consult your notes or a speaker before printing.

**Action — Editing Pass 2: Translation Accuracy**

* Does the English translation make sense on its own?  
* Does the literal translation appear first, even if it sounds unusual in English?  
* Are there Mandinka words that cannot be translated — words that must remain in Mandinka with a brief explanation?

**Action — Editing Pass 3: Meaning Clarity**

* Can a ten-year-old reader understand what this proverb teaches?  
* Does the meaning statement stay true to what the elder or community intended?  
* Does any sentence accidentally oversimplify or misrepresent the cultural meaning?
