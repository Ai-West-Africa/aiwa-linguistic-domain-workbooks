# AI West Africa Manuscript Preparation Workbook

## Mandinka Birds — Author's Proof Edition (AIWA Junior Publishing Fellowship)

**Publisher Context:** AI West Africa — An African Owned, Busumbala Born, Gambian Grown Company.

## Project Summary: Legal and Ethical Framing

This workbook uses a prospective, student-author model for contemporary educational publishing.

- **Non-Extractive:** We do not remove raw heritage data from the community.
- **Value-Added:** Students produce derivative educational works that are legally distinct and serve public education.
- **Community-Owned:** The physical workbook remains in the school library and preserves community intellectual value.

**Legal Narrative:** Legacy records are used only as creative triggers. Students retain moral and economic rights to their original contemporary contributions, which may be submitted to a sovereign knowledge platform for royalty-bearing publication.

## Workbook Positioning: Personal Lab vs Sovereign Product

- **Personal Lab (Workbook):** Learning notes, interviews, lexical discovery, field observations, media notes, and draft experiments.
- **Sovereign Product (Platform Submission):** Polished manuscript pages authored by the student as rights-holder.

Address the student as **Author and Rights-Holder**. Record findings, then produce master text for publication.

## Fellowship Use in School

This workbook is designed to function as a real 7-week publishing fellowship, a language preservation toolkit, and a classroom field guide.

- It supports UNESCO-aligned language preservation by linking ecological knowledge, oral tradition, and linguistic memory.
- It supports UNESCO intangible cultural heritage concepts by treating proverb, metaphor, naming, and oral interpretation as living knowledge.
- It supports MoBSE language goals by helping students create local-language educational resources rooted in Gambian life.
- It supports literacy development through observation, interviewing, terminology work, story-making, and youth authorship.

## Teacher/Facilitator Orientation

- Teach the bird domain as both science and ancestral intelligence.
- Protect Mandinka naming, metaphor, and oral interpretation alongside observation and classification.
- Use the workbook as a production tool for evidence-based publishing, not as a disconnected worksheet packet.
- Keep every week moving from listening, to noticing, to documenting, to creating, to publishing.
- Help students understand that a bird can be at once species, sign, memory carrier, and teaching metaphor.

## Week 2: Domain Immersion and Foundational Knowledge

### Week 2 UNESCO Ethics Reminder

Before continuing editorial research, repeat the **Knowledge Exchange** with contributors and confirm consent for educational publication, attribution, and classroom circulation.

### Foundational Knowledge Focus: What the Bird Domain Carries

Use this week to establish the deeper Mandinka logic around birds.

- **Birds in oral tradition:** bird voices, bird images, and bird behavior appear in proverb, praise, warning, and teaching story.
- **Birds in ecology:** habitat, season, nesting, feeding, and migration are part of community observation.
- **Birds in social meaning:** some birds signal patience, danger, kingship, hospitality, foolishness, or endurance.
- **Birds in ancestral intelligence:** hunters, farmers, jalis, matriarchs, and youth each read birds differently.
- **Birds in education:** bird knowledge can become vocabulary, science learning, environmental care, and cultural pride.

#### Week 2 Teacher Guidance

- Let students speak both from observation and from inherited memory.
- Encourage them to notice when one bird carries more than one meaning.
- Use the week to prepare students to move confidently between field observation and oral interpretation.

#### Week 2 Domain Immersion Activities

1. Build a bird knowledge map linking species, Mandinka name, habitat, proverb, and social meaning.
2. Record one note on how a bird functions as messenger, warning, or teaching sign in community life.
3. Write one short comparison between a scientific description and a Mandinka cultural description of the same bird.
4. Identify one bird that younger learners in Gambian schools should know first, and explain why.

## Week 3: Language Structure, Grammar, Sentence Engineering, and Terminology

### Chapter 2 UNESCO Ethics Clause (Mandatory)

Before continuing editorial research, repeat the **Knowledge Exchange** with contributors and confirm consent for educational publication, attribution, and classroom circulation.

### Chapter 2: Editorial Research Lexicon and Scientific Field Guide Entries

**Objective:** Build chapter-ready lexical entries as textbook assets.

#### Week 3 Teacher Guidance

- Keep exact names and oral explanations before students attempt school-style definitions.
- Show students how sentence engineering helps them move from oral field note to educational paragraph.
- Teach that translation is not only about "what bird is this?" but also "what does this bird mean here?"
- Keep local naming and descriptive logic visible beside scientific description.

#### Sentence Engineering Studio

Students should practice bird language through the following frames:

- **Nouns:** bird names, body parts, habitats, sounds, and places
- **Pronouns:** possession, relation, and speaker perspective
- **Verbs:** flying, perching, calling, warning, nesting, hunting, arriving
- **Adjectives and adverbs:** color, size, movement, intensity, timing
- **Position and time words:** where birds appear and when signs matter
- **Negation:** what a bird does not do, or what a sign does not mean
- **Respect and interpretation words:** caution, blessing, taboo, proverb use
- **Sentence type:** statement, question, instruction, warning, story line, proverb line

#### Chapter 2 Editorial Assignment Activities

1. Create 10 scientific field-guide entries for selected birds: identification markers, habitat, Mandinka name, and educational note.
2. For each entry, write one "Inspired by" line (heritage context, sepia/black-and-white layout zone) and one "New Work" line (modern student-authored explanation, high-color layout zone).
3. Record your finding: which entry best demonstrates cultural continuity and modern relevance?
4. Write three sentence versions for one bird: field note, classroom explanation, and younger-reader explanation.
5. Mark where exact pronunciation, tone, or metaphor changes the educational meaning.

#### Terminology Integrity Checklist

- Record the exact bird name first.
- Note variant pronunciations or local alternatives.
- Distinguish direct observation from proverb or metaphor use.
- Mark whether the line is species description, social meaning, taboo, or praise image.
- Add a note where English labeling is helpful but incomplete.

## Week 4: Interview Preparation, Oral History Method, and Fieldwork Practice

### Week 4 UNESCO Ethics Clause (Mandatory)

Complete the **Knowledge Exchange** before any audio, image, or interview practice. Confirm that recording, playback, and classroom use are consented and context-safe.

### Interview Preparation Studio

**Objective:** Prepare students to gather bird knowledge respectfully, accurately, and professionally.

#### Week 4 Teacher Guidance

- Rehearse before going to the field.
- Show students that a good bird interview moves from simple observation to deep meaning.
- Assign team roles so listening quality stays high.
- Remind students that pauses, gestures, laughter, and sound imitation are part of the data.

#### Team Roles for Practice Interviews

- **Lead interviewer:** opens, asks, and closes respectfully
- **Consent recorder:** confirms what may be written, recorded, or photographed
- **Language scribe:** captures exact wording, names, and pronunciation notes
- **Observation recorder:** records setting, habitat clues, gestures, sound imitation, and emotional tone

#### Question Sequencing Guide

1. **Warm opening:** greeting, gratitude, and purpose
2. **Observation question:** "Which bird do you notice first in this season or place?"
3. **Meaning question:** "What does this bird tell the community?"
4. **Story question:** ask for proverb, folktale, memory, or warning
5. **Follow-up question:** ask for another example, different season, or another speaker group
6. **Clarification question:** ask what younger learners usually misunderstand
7. **Closing question:** ask what should be preserved exactly in the book

#### Respectful Elder Engagement Checklist

- Greet properly and let the elder settle before questions begin.
- Ask where the conversation should happen and whether a sound recording is comfortable there.
- Do not interrupt a story, proverb, chant, or bird-call imitation.
- Ask before replaying bird audio or showing photos.
- Thank the elder again after the interview and repeat back any restrictions.

#### Informed Consent Explanation Frame

"We are preparing a Mandinka educational workbook for school and community learning. We want to preserve bird knowledge, names, stories, and meanings with respect. We would like your permission to listen, take notes, and, if you agree, record your voice or examples. You may tell us what can be written, what should stay private, and what should be checked again before publication."

#### Observation and Note-Taking Guide

- Write the exact word first, then the explanation.
- Note the place, time, season, weather, and bird behavior connected to the answer.
- Mark whether the speaker is describing species knowledge, proverb meaning, taboo, or life lesson.
- Separate what you saw, what you heard, and what you inferred.
- Record emotional significance: amusement, warning, pride, grief, or reverence.

#### Pronunciation, Nuance, and Meaning Capture

- Ask for repetition of key names if needed.
- Record sound imitation separately from ordinary speech.
- Note whether the bird name is descriptive, onomatopoeic, or lineage-linked.
- Ask if younger speakers still use the same term.

#### Handling Uncertainty Respectfully

- If the answer is unclear, ask for another scene or example rather than forcing a quick definition.
- If accounts conflict, record all versions with speaker identity and context.
- Use triangulation across hunter, farmer, jali, matriarch, and youth perspectives.
- Preserve exact wording even when interpretation remains open.

#### Interview Preparation Assignment Activities

1. Draft a question bank for at least four speaker types: elder, farmer, jali, and matriarch.
2. Rehearse a 3-minute consent explanation in pairs.
3. Practice identifying metaphor, proverb meaning, and emotional significance from a sample oral response.
4. Prepare a field notebook page with quotation space, habitat notes, pronunciation notes, and verification notes.

## Week 5: Field Interviews and Evidence Gathering

### Week 5 UNESCO Ethics Clause (Mandatory)

Before any recording begins, restate the **Knowledge Exchange** and confirm the elder's final comfort with the interview, audio, and image process.

### Fieldwork Practice: Living Bird Evidence

**Objective:** Gather accurate field evidence that can become educational material without losing cultural depth.

#### Week 5 Teacher Guidance

- Keep teams small and purposeful.
- Pair field observation with oral explanation wherever possible.
- Debrief immediately after each session while memory is fresh.
- Require students to separate exact quotation, observation, interpretation, and later educational use.

#### Field Interview Workflow

1. Greet and settle.
2. Restate purpose and permissions.
3. Start with familiar birds, local places, or seasonal signs.
4. Move gradually into proverb, taboo, moral, and memory.
5. Ask follow-up questions on sound, movement, habitat, and emotional meaning.
6. Close by confirming what may be quoted and how the elder wishes to be credited.

#### Evidence Gathering Priorities

- Exact bird names and variants
- Habitat and behavior observations
- Sound descriptions or recorded calls
- Proverbs, metaphor lines, warnings, or praise references
- Emotional significance and social context
- Conflicting accounts that require triangulation
- Notes on what is best suited for younger readers and school use

### NEW Major Section: Concepting and Creating Educational Children's Books

**Purpose:** Turn bird fieldwork into children's books that older students create for younger students.

#### Week 6 Teacher Guidance

- Remind students that children's publishing must remain culturally grounded and observant, not shallow.
- Help them keep both ecological truth and cultural meaning in view.
- Encourage books that support local-language education and curiosity about the environment.
- Use page planning and visual notes so the story can genuinely be illustrated and taught.

#### Children's Book Concepting Steps

1. **Choose one strong fieldwork truth:** a bird as messenger, helper, warning, season marker, or proverb carrier.
2. **Identify the young reader:** lower basic, upper basic, or early independent reader.
3. **Set the educational purpose:** vocabulary learning, environmental awareness, listening skills, moral reflection, or cultural memory.
4. **Use age-appropriate language:** short sentences, repeated key terms, and clear scenes.
5. **Protect Mandinka meaning:** when a term cannot be simplified, explain it through image, sound, or action instead of replacing it.

#### Educational Storytelling Basics

- Open with a recognizable Gambian setting.
- Introduce the bird through sight, sound, or family talk.
- Bring in an elder voice, proverb, or remembered lesson naturally.
- End with a clear feeling of continuity, care, or understanding.

#### Page Planning and Visual Storytelling

- Page 1-2: setting, child viewpoint, and first bird encounter
- Page 3-6: observation, question, or problem
- Page 7-10: elder explanation, proverb, or discovery
- Page 11-12: closing lesson, glossary word, or invitation to notice birds differently

#### Illustration Planning Notes

- Show real compounds, fields, mangroves, rice areas, trees, and school settings from Gambian life.
- Use illustrations to support identification: beak, color, posture, nest, or movement.
- Keep symbols and emotional cues local and recognizable.
- Add notes where sound, silence, or group listening must be visible on the page.

#### Educational Messaging Check

- Does the book support local-language education?
- Does it strengthen cultural pride without losing observational truth?
- Can a teacher use it for literacy, vocabulary, and discussion?
- Does it help a younger learner see why birds matter in Mandinka life?

### Chapter 4: Manuscript Assembly and Sovereign Submission Bridge

**Objective:** Convert drafts into publishable educational materials.

#### Week 7 Teacher Guidance

- Teach editing as a final act of care for language, source, and reader.
- Review both factual accuracy and educational usability.
- Make sure the final product still sounds grounded in Gambian and Mandinka life.
- Help students understand that archiving is part of preservation, not an afterthought.

#### Week 7 Editorial Assignment Activities

1. Assemble chapter sections: inspired heritage notes, lexical entries, media plates, new authored prose, and children's book concept pages.
